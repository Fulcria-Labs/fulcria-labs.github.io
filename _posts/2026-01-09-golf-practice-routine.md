Most golfers' range sessions are wasted effort. They dump a bucket of balls, aim at the 150 marker, and mindlessly hit until the bucket's empty. Zero improvement.

Studies show the average golfer hits 50-100 balls per range session but retains almost none of the changes they're "working on." The problem isn't effort—it's approach.

Here's a 90-second routine that builds real skills in minimal time.

## Why 90 Seconds?

Attention and intention matter more than volume. Research on motor learning shows that focused, deliberate practice beats mindless repetition by a factor of 10.

**One intentional swing with clear focus beats 10 autopilot swings.**

The science backs this up:
- **Blocked practice** (hitting the same shot repeatedly) feels productive but produces poor retention
- **Varied practice** (changing targets, clubs, intentions) creates stronger neural pathways
- **Spacing** (rest between attempts) allows the brain to encode learning

The 90-second routine incorporates all three principles.

## The Routine

### 10 seconds: Pick ONE focus

Not two things. One. Your brain can only consciously manage one swing thought effectively. Examples:

- Keep my head still through impact
- Feel my [weight shift to front foot](/blog/golf-weight-transfer/)
- Pause at the top of my [backswing](/blog/golf-backswing/) ([tempo fundamentals](/blog/golf-swing-tempo/))
- Complete [follow-through to balanced finish](/blog/golf-swing-follow-through/)
- Feel the [clubface square at impact](/blog/golf-club-face-control/)

**Pro tip:** Write your focus on a notecard in your pocket. When fatigue sets in (usually around shot 30), you'll drift back to old habits. The card brings you back.

### 20 seconds: Visualize the shot

See the ball flight in your mind. Where's it starting? What shape is it taking? Where's it landing?

Visualization isn't woo-woo—it's neuroscience. Studies show mental rehearsal activates the same motor neurons as physical practice. Tour pros spend nearly as much time visualizing as hitting.

Be specific:
- Pick an exact target (not "the 150 marker"—the left edge of the 150 sign)
- See the trajectory (low draw, high fade, straight bullet)
- Imagine the landing and roll

### 10 seconds: Practice swing

One rehearsal swing focusing on your key thought. This isn't a warm-up—it's a motor pattern primer.

Make this practice swing intentional. Feel the specific sensation you're trying to create. Many golfers rush through practice swings. Don't.

### 10 seconds: Execute

Hit the ball with the same intention.

**Critical:** Don't add anything at the last second. Your practice swing was the plan. Execute the plan.

### 40 seconds: Evaluate

What happened? Did you achieve your focus? What was the result?

Don't immediately hit another ball. **The learning happens in the evaluation.**

Ask yourself:
1. Did I execute my focus? (yes/no)
2. What was the ball flight?
3. What did I feel in my body?
4. What would I do differently?

This evaluation phase is where most golfers fail. They hit, curse, and immediately tee up another ball. No learning occurs.

## Common Mistakes That Kill Progress

### Mistake 1: Too many swing thoughts
Your friend says "turn your hips." The golf channel says "shallow the club." YouTube says "bump the hips laterally." You try all three simultaneously. Result: paralysis.

**Fix:** One thought per session. Rotate focuses across sessions.

### Mistake 2: Outcome focus
You're fixated on whether the ball went straight. But the ball doesn't know your intention. A pure push can result from a dozen different swing flaws.

**Fix:** Evaluate execution, not outcome. Did you DO the thing you intended? That's the only question.

### Mistake 3: Volume addiction
Hitting more balls feels productive. It's not. After 40-50 focused shots, most amateurs' quality degrades significantly.

**Fix:** Quality over quantity. 30 deliberate shots > 100 mindless ones.

### Mistake 4: No rest between shots
Machine-gunning balls prevents consolidation. Your brain needs processing time.

**Fix:** The 40-second evaluation isn't optional. Use it.

## Building a Full Session

Repeat this 90-second cycle 10 times = 15 minutes of focused practice.

That's more valuable than an hour of mindless hitting.

For a complete 45-minute session:
- **Warm-up (10 min):** Half swings, gradually building to full
- **Focus block 1 (15 min):** 10 cycles with one swing thought
- **Variety block (10 min):** Change clubs and targets each shot
- **Focus block 2 (10 min):** Return to original thought or introduce new one

## Weekly Practice Structure

For consistent improvement, aim for 2-3 focused sessions per week:

**Day 1: Full swing focus**
- 30-40 shots using the 90-second routine
- Focus: One technical element (e.g., [transition](/blog/golf-swing-transition/))

**Day 2: Short game**
- Chipping, pitching, putting
- Apply same deliberate practice principles

**Day 3: On-course practice**
- Play 9 holes focusing on [course management](/blog/golf-course-management/)
- One shot at a time, full routine each time

## Track Your Progress

Keep a simple practice journal:
- Date
- Focus for the day
- Number of quality shots
- Key insight or feeling discovered
- Grade yourself (A-F)

After a month, you'll see patterns. Maybe your [balance drills](/blog/golf-balance-drills/) always produce A sessions while your [lag work](/blog/golf-lag-guide/) produces C sessions. That's useful information.
